# 10 — Arabic content style

Clear Arabic content is part of trust. Write for citizens completing a government service.

## Arabic UX writing style

- Plain, formal, respectful Arabic. Clear over clever.
- Address the user directly and consistently; keep tone calm and official.
- Be concise: short sentences, one idea per line where possible.

## Clear government service language

- Use the official, commonly understood term for each service, action, and status.
- Be specific about what the user must do and what will happen next.
- Avoid jargon and internal/system terminology in user-facing text.

## Direct verbs

- Start actions with a clear verb: ابدأ، أرسل، حمّل، تحقّق، راجِع، أكِّد.
- Match button labels to the action they perform; avoid generic labels.
- Keep verb forms consistent across the product.

## Short labels

- Field and navigation labels are short and unambiguous.
- Prefer a few precise words over a long phrase.
- Keep terminology consistent (the same thing has the same name everywhere).

## Error messages

- Say what went wrong and how to fix it, specifically and politely.
- Avoid blame and avoid vague text like "حدث خطأ".
- Don't expose technical details, codes, or stack traces to the user.

## Success messages

- Confirm what succeeded and what the user can do next.
- Reserve strong success styling (and the success color) for genuine positive outcomes.
- Include any reference/identifier the user should keep.

## Status messages

- Use a consistent vocabulary for states (e.g., قيد المراجعة، مكتمل، مرفوض، غير متاح).
- Pair status text with an icon and consistent color mapping.
- Keep status wording stable across screens and components.

## Avoid literal technical translation

- Translate meaning, not words; avoid word-for-word renderings that read awkwardly in Arabic.
- Adapt phrasing to natural Arabic usage and government context.
- Review machine-translated strings before shipping.

## Keep English product names readable

- Preserve Latin product/brand names and technical identifiers in their original form.
- Isolate them within Arabic text so order stays correct (see RTL guidance).
- Don't transliterate or mirror names unless that is the intended content.
